PATIENT PROFILE: DAD

PATIENT
My father, mid-80s.

This profile contains patient facts, care-plan limits, food preferences, and caregiver context. The application recipe audit prompt remains the governing instruction layer for recipe review, recipe correction, and final caregiver output.

PRIMARY DIAGNOSES
- Parkinson's disease, managed with carbidopa-levodopa.
- Chronic Lymphocytic Leukemia, under active treatment with Brukinsa.
- Chronic Kidney Disease.
- Hyperkalemia risk. Lokelma use active.
- Coronary artery disease, 8 cardiac stents, history of 2 heart attacks.
- Orthostatic blood pressure instability.
- Severe bilateral knee osteoarthritis, bone-on-bone.
- Lumbar spinal stenosis.
- Prostate cancer, in remission.
- Recent severe dehydration leading to renal failure and a fall. This event drives the hydration plan.

CURRENT MEDICATIONS
- Brukinsa, zanubrutinib, for CLL.
- Carbidopa-Levodopa for Parkinson's disease.
- Levodopa dosing times: 6:30am, 12:00pm, 5:30pm.
- Lamotrigine, taken at levodopa cycles.
- Gabapentin, taken at levodopa cycles.
- Atorvastatin, statin. Large amounts of grapefruit create medication concern.
- Pantoprazole, PPI for gastrointestinal protection.
- Aspirin, daily cardioprotective use.
- Nitroglycerin, PRN for chest discomfort.
- Lokelma, sodium zirconium cyclosilicate, for hyperkalemia. Potassium restriction is active.
- Medication name needing clarification: caregiver listed one medication as "Medicine." Replace this with actual drug name and purpose before using it in recipe logic.

ACTIVE DAILY LIMITS
- Sodium ceiling: 2400 mg per day.
- Potassium ceiling: 1800 mg per day.

Use mg for milligrams. Avoid Mg because Mg means magnesium.

PROTEIN AND LEVODOPA TIMING
Levodopa cycles:
- 6:30am
- 12:00pm
- 5:30pm

Meal rhythm:
- Breakfast around 7:15am: moderate protein.
- Lunch around 12:45pm: substantial protein, often the day's largest protein meal.
- Dinner around 6:15pm: balanced protein.

Protein meals generally land 45 to 60 minutes after the relevant levodopa dose when appetite allows. When appetite is fragile, getting food in him takes priority.

HYDRATION PLAN
Hydration is a top priority because of the recent dehydration, renal failure, and fall.

Every meal and snack pairs with a cold liquid placed on the table before the plate arrives.

He strongly resists plain water as the only fluid.

Accepted hydration options:
- Coffee, preferred.
- Diluted juice, half juice and half water.
- Diluted tart cherry juice when potassium budget allows.
- Diluted cranberry juice.
- Low-sodium broth when appropriate.
- Milk with meals when appropriate.
- Hydrating foods such as cucumber, watermelon, berries, citrus segments, and soups, with potassium budget considered.

POTASSIUM CONTEXT
Potassium restriction is active because of CKD, hyperkalemia risk, and Lokelma use.

Daily potassium ceiling: 1800 mg.

Foods that require portion control or substitution based on day-level potassium budget:
- Avocado.
- Banana.
- Potato.
- Sweet potato.
- Yam.
- Orange or large citrus portions.
- Spinach in volume.
- Dried fruit.
- Tomato paste, tomato sauce, and sun-dried tomato in volume.
- Beans and legumes.
- Squash.
- Large portions of high-potassium fruits or vegetables.

Lower-potassium plate structure often uses:
- White rice.
- Pasta.
- Couscous.
- Bread or tortillas.
- Cucumber.
- Bell pepper.
- Green beans.
- Lettuce.
- Small portions of berries.
- Carefully portioned soft fruits.

SODIUM CONTEXT
Daily sodium ceiling: 2400 mg.

Sodium matters because of CKD, coronary artery disease, blood pressure volatility, and cardiac history.

Foods that require caution:
- Deli meats.
- Cured meats.
- Canned soups.
- Packaged sauces.
- Soy sauce.
- Fish sauce.
- Oyster sauce.
- Bottled marinades.
- Processed cheese.
- Restaurant foods.
- Large amounts of broth, even when labeled lower sodium.

Preferred approach:
- Use measured salt.
- Use unsalted or truly low-sodium broth.
- Use small measured amounts of high-impact sauces when they carry the dish.
- Rebuild flavor with acid, herbs, aromatics, browning, olive oil, pan fond, and texture.

PHOSPHORUS CONTEXT
CKD creates phosphorus concern.

Limit or watch:
- Cola and dark sodas.
- Processed cheese.
- Processed meats.
- Packaged baked goods with phosphate additives.
- Large dairy portions.
- Large whole-grain portions when phosphorus budget matters.

BLEEDING AND INFECTION CONTEXT
Brukinsa plus aspirin creates elevated bleeding concern.

This affects recipe handling mostly through:
- Food safety.
- Clean prep.
- Fully cooked proteins.
- Avoiding risky raw or undercooked foods.
- Avoiding sloppy knife work or hard-to-manage prep during caregiver fatigue.

TASTE PERCEPTION
Metallic taste risk is high because of CKD, CLL treatment, and medications.

Garlic triggers metallic taste for him.

Flavor approaches that work:
- Citrus zest.
- Lemon juice.
- Lime juice.
- Fresh herbs.
- Olive oil.
- Mushrooms when potassium budget allows.
- Mild vinegar.
- Modest tomato.
- Browning and searing.
- Warm, aromatic sauces.
- Plastic or bamboo utensils when metallic taste is active.
- Salt-water or baking-soda mouth rinse before meals when tolerated.
- Slightly cooler serving temperature rather than very hot food.

TEXTURE AND EATING FATIGUE
Tremor, Parkinson's symptoms, orthopedic pain, and meal fatigue affect intake.

Preferred textures:
- Rice bowls.
- Soft proteins.
- Eggs.
- Fish.
- Ground meats.
- Slow-cooked chicken.
- Yogurt.
- Soups.
- Roasted-soft vegetables.
- Soft fruits.
- Bite-sized pieces.
- Moist foods with sauce.

APPETITE MODES
Every adapted recipe needs three modes:
- Strong appetite.
- Neutral appetite.
- Low appetite.

Strong appetite:
- Add calories mainly through starch, oil, sauce, or caregiver-approved sides before adding extra protein or potassium-heavy foods.

Neutral appetite:
- Use the recipe as written after adaptation.

Low appetite:
- Smaller plate.
- Smaller visual portion.
- Aroma-forward plating.
- Tender protein.
- Moist starch.
- Hydration visible first.
- Food that looks manageable.

Aroma matters:
- Browning.
- Searing.
- Toasting.
- Fresh herbs.
- Citrus.
- Warm sauce.
- Familiar comfort cues.

- 9:30am: midmorning snack plus fluids.

CAREGIVER GOAL

Recipes must be executable in a home kitchen by his wife and son.

The best output is practical, flavorful, safe enough, and likely to be eaten.
